ABSTRACT

This graduation project proposes the development of an integrated Smart Rehabilitation Glove, an innovative wearable device engineered to enhance hand rehabilitation protocols. Key highlights include:

Objective: To assist patients with neurological and musculoskeletal impairments in recovering hand functionality through integrated therapeutic modules and objective performance tracking.
System Architecture: The glove integrates three sophisticated modules:
TENS Module: Manual control for nerve stimulation to modulate pain and mitigate muscle spasticity.
Vibration Therapy: Manual control for sensory stimulation to enhance neuromuscular responses.
Force Measurement: High-precision sensors providing real-time digital feedback on grip strength.
Clinical Value: The system functions as a comprehensive diagnostic and therapeutic tool. It empowers therapists to monitor patients’ progress quantitatively and adjust treatment intensity manually based on objective data, ensuring a personalized and documented recovery path.

 INTRODUCTION







INTRODUCTION

Problem Statement

The Core Challenge of Hand Rehabilitation

The fundamental problem in hand rehabilitation lies in overcoming the functional and physical deficits that arise from injuries, diseases, or neurological conditions affecting the hand and upper extremity. The ultimate goal is to restore—or compensate for—lost capabilities, enabling individuals to carry out everyday activities, regain their autonomy, and enhance their overall quality of life.

The key difficulties encountered in this process can be summarized as follows:


📌 Limited Range of Motion (ROM)

Fractures and arthritis restrict joint mobility, directly impairing the ability to grasp, pinch, or perform essential movements.


📌 Muscle Atrophy & Weakness
Prolonged immobilization leads to muscle wasting in the hand and forearm, significantly reducing grip strength and endurance.


📌 Sensory Deficits
Nerve damage alters or diminishes tactile feedback (touch, temperature, pressure), making safe object manipulation difficult.


📌 Impaired Coordination & Dexterity

Disrupted motor skills make fine, precise tasks—such as writing, typing, or handling small objects—extremely challenging.


📌 Chronic Pain Management
Persistent pain acts as a major barrier, preventing patients from utilizing their hand effectively in daily routines


📌 Psychosocial Impact
Hand disabilities often lead to emotional distress, affecting self-esteem, confidence, and social integration.

💡 Conclusion

Hand rehabilitation focuses on designing and executing tailored, multi-disciplinary strategies—combining therapeutic exercises, assistive technologies, and medical interventions—to overcome these barriers and optimize hand function.





1.2 Historical Background


The evolution of hand rehabilitation spans many centuries, shaped by continuous medical progress and shifting treatment philosophies.

Ancient Beginnings: Early societies understood the critical role of hand function and devised primitive therapeutic methods. In ancient Egypt, for instance, historical records indicate the use of splinting and joint movement exercises to manage hand injuries and ailments.

The 18th and 19th Centuries: This era witnessed major strides in both surgical intervention and postsurgical recovery. French physician Guillaume Dupuytren pioneered operative procedures for correcting hand deformities, establishing principles that would later underpin modern rehabilitation approaches.

Impact of the World Wars: The severe trauma experienced by soldiers during both World Wars catalyzed rapid innovation in the field. Visionary surgeons like George E. Omer and Sterling Bunnell introduced groundbreaking methods—including tendon rerouting and custom splint fabrication—that dramatically advanced treatment possibilities.

Mid-20th Century Recognition: The 1950s and 1960s saw hand rehabilitation emerge as a formal specialty. A key turning point came in 1977 with the founding of the American Society of Hand Therapists (ASHT), which solidified the profession's identity and standards.

Technological Breakthroughs: Modern devices have transformed patient care. The arrival of advanced prosthetic limbs, customized orthoses, and functional electrical stimulation (FES) has broadened therapeutic choices and significantly enhanced recovery outcomes for those with hand impairments.

Rise of Evidence-Based Care: Over the past few decades, the field has increasingly relied on scientific validation. Clinical studies and systematic research have clarified which treatment strategies are most effective, guiding clinical decision-making with solid data.

Team-Based Approach: Contemporary practice now emphasizes multidisciplinary cooperation. Hand surgeons, occupational and physical therapists, and other specialists work together to ensure thorough assessments, coordinated care plans, and long-term follow-up for patients.

Ongoing Progress: Present-day hand rehabilitation continues to evolve through active research, technological refinement, and personalized care strategies. Its enduring mission is to restore hand functionality, foster patient autonomy, and enhance the overall well-being of those affected by hand injuries, chronic conditions, or disabilities.

Chapter Three










Materials and Methods




Materials and Methods



1. Display Screen


Figure 2


Function: 
The visual user interface that shows real-time system data such as pressure levels, force sensor readings, valve status (open/closed), vibration intensity, and system diagnostics. It provides feedback to the user or operator about what the glove is currently experiencing.



2. Power Supply Adaptor



Figure 3

Function: 
Converts AC mains power (220V) to DC voltage (12V or 5V) to power all electronic components in the device. Protects the circuit from voltage fluctuations.


3. ESP32 (Controller)

 
Figure 4

Function: 
The brain of the system. This microcontroller reads data from sensors (force sensors), processes it, makes decisions, and sends control signals to the valves, vibration motors, and display. It can also handle wireless communication (Wi-Fi/Bluetooth) if needed.






4. Pneumatic Glove




Figure 5


Function: 
The wearable component that the patient puts on their hand. Contains pneumatic actuators (air tubes) that inflate and deflate to assist with finger opening and closing. Used for stroke patient rehabilitation.



5. MOSFET Module


Figure 6

Function: 
An electronic high-speed switch. The ESP32 cannot directly drive high-current devices like vibration motors or valves. The MOSFET module allows the low-voltage ESP32 signal to control a high-current load safely. It also enables PWM (Pulse Width Modulation) for speed/intensity control.



6. Power Supply Module



Figure 7

Function: 
A voltage regulation and distribution board. It takes the single input from the adaptor and splits/regulates it into multiple stable output voltages (e.g., 3.3V for ESP32, 5V for sensors, 12V for valves) to match each component's needs. It also provides overcurrent protection.




7. TENS Cable


Figure 8

Function:
 Transmission of Electrical Impulses: The cable acts as a conduit to transfer electrical pulses (voltage) directly from the TENS device to the human body.

Targeted Muscle Stimulation: It delivers these impulses specifically to the muscle area that you want to stimulate, activate, or exercise.




8. Vibration Motor


Figure 9




Function: 
A haptic actuator that produces physical vibrations at specific locations on the glove (usually fingertips or palm). It provides tactile feedback simulating texture, friction, vibration, or impact to enhance the user's sense of touch.




9. Force Sensor



Figure 10

Function: 
A thin, flexible sensor placed on the fingertips or palm of the glove. It measures how much pressure or forces the user is applying (or receiving). This data is sent to the ESP32 to adjust the pneumatic response and vibration intensity accordingly.







10. Valves



Figure 11

Function: 
Electromechanical air valves that control the flow of compressed air into and out of the glove's air chambers. The ESP32 sends signals to open/close them, allowing precise inflation and deflation to create pressure sensations.




11. Control Panel


Function:
The physical user interface with buttons, switches, and LEDs. It allows the user to power the system on/off, select operating modes, adjust intensity levels, and see basic status indicators without using the software display.




12. Voltage Regulator 7805



Figure 12
Function: 
A linear voltage regulator IC that converts a higher input voltage (e.g., 7V–35V) into a stable, fixed +5V DC output. It is used to power 5V-sensitive components like the ESP32, HX710B sensor, and display screen. It provides clean, ripple-free voltage compared to switching regulators.



13. Air Pressure Sensor HX710B


Figure 13



Function: 
A digital air pressure sensor module that measures pneumatic pressure (0–40 kPa range) inside the glove's air chambers. It uses the HX710B 24-bit ADC chip to convert the analog pressure signal into a digital output readable by the ESP32 via a serial interface (SCK/OUT pins). This enables closed-loop pressure control for precise haptic feedback.



Development Methodology:
The development process followed a systematic top-down design and bottom-up integration approach, implemented through the following sequential phases:

Phase One: Analysis and Requirements Specification
This phase involved defining the functional and non-functional requirements of the system in consultation with physiotherapy reference sources. Key parameters were established, including the required range of motion for the fingers, the necessary actuation force levels, and the rehabilitation exercise scenarios (passive, active-assistive, and resistive modes). Operational specifications for all components—such as pneumatic operating pressure, actuator power ratings, and sensor accuracy—were determined based on these clinical and technical requirements.

Phase Two: Theoretical Design
The system architecture was decomposed into four primary layers:
Sensing Layer: Comprising the force sensors and vibration motors.
Processing and Control Layer: Centered around the ESP 32 microcontroller and the control panel.
Actuation and Power Layer: Including the pneumatic valves, pneumatic glove, and MOSFET switching modules.
Feedback and Interface Layer: Incorporating the display screen for real-time data visualization.
Comprehensive block diagrams were constructed, and communication protocols (GPIO, PWM, UART) were defined for interlayer data exchange.

Phase Three: Modular Assembly and Unit Testing
To facilitate systematic debugging and validation, the system was developed in independent modular stages:
Power Supply Testing: The power supply adaptor and power supply module were assembled and tested to verify stable voltage outputs (5V, 3.3V, and 12V) under no-load and partial-load conditions before connection to sensitive components.
Control Circuit Testing: The ESP 32 was programmed to perform basic I/O operations, including LED indicators and vibration motor activation, while force sensor readings were validated through the ADC (Analog-to-Digital Converter) interface.
Pneumatic Actuation Testing: Solenoid valves and MOSFET modules were connected to a prototype pneumatic glove. Inflation and deflation cycles were tested to simulate finger flexion and extension, with timing parameters adjusted for smooth motion.
Feedback Interface Testing: The display screen was interfaced to confirm real-time graphical and numerical representation of sensed data.

Phase Four: Full System Integration
Following successful unit validation, all components were physically assembled into the final mechanical housing (glove, control panel, and electronic/pneumatic enclosure). Tenz cables were employed for reliable signal transmission between the stationary control unit and the moving glove assembly. The unified embedded firmware was developed to manage all system tasks concurrently using ESP 32 timers and interrupt routines, ensuring synchronized operation of sensing, actuation, and display functions.

Phase Five: Functional Verification and System Validation
The fully integrated system was subjected to operational testing under conditions simulating real-world clinical use. The following performance metrics were evaluated:
Response time of pneumatic valves to control signals from the microcontroller.
Accuracy and linearity of force sensor readings under varying applied loads.
System stability during prolonged rehabilitation sessions (multiple repeated cycles), monitoring for overheating, voltage drops, or communication failures.

Phase Six: Calibration and Final Tuning
Based on the validation results, iterative calibration procedures were performed. Force sensor offset and gain values were adjusted to minimize measurement errors, and valve timing parameters were fine-tuned to achieve smooth, natural finger movement trajectories. The display screen interface was also optimized to present the most clinically relevant parameters—such as grip force, finger angle, and repetition count—in a clear and accessible format for both therapist and patient.

Optional Addition (if applicable):
The embedded firmware was developed using the Arduino Integrated Development Environment (IDE) with C++ programming language, while mechanical design and housing fabrication were supported by CAD software for structural layout planning.














Chapter Four










 Results





Results

4.1 Architecture Overview and Design Philosophy

The primary objective of this project is the development of an intelligent, wearable mechatronic glove capable of closed-loop sensing and multi-modal haptic feedback. The system architecture follows a decentralized approach divided into three distinct functional domains:

1. Processing and Control Hub: Managed by a dual-core ESP32 microcontroller, which orchestrates sensor data acquisition, computes control loops, and handles wireless telemetry.

2. Input and Sensing Subsystem: Utilizes resistive force grids to detect physical user intent and interaction boundaries.

3. Multi-Modal Actuation and Feedback Network: Combines macro-scale pneumatic muscle inflation, micro-scale mechanical vibration, and electro-tactile Transcutaneous Electrical Nerve Stimulation (TENS) to simulate complex physical sensations.

4.2 Detailed Hardware Component Specifications

Core Controller: ESP32 Development Platform

The system is controlled by the ESP-WROOM-32 platform. It provides the necessary computing power to run sensory filters and actuation modulators simultaneously:
Dual-Core Tensilica LX6: Allows core-level isolation. Core 1 is dedicated to time-critical analog sampling and safety checks, while Core 0 handles Wi-Fi data transmission and PWM generation.

Peripheral Mapping: The extensive I/O layout allows direct interfacing with multiple analog-to-digital converters (ADC) and dedicated hardware PWM channels.

Power Management and Distribution

Operating a mixed-signal system containing both low-voltage logic (3.3V) and high-current inductive/high-voltage loads requires rigid power isolation:

DC-DC Step-Down (Buck) Conversion: An LM2596-based buck converter module stabilizes the variable high-voltage input down to a steady SV bus. The onboard multi-tum potentiometer is adjusted counter-clockwise to achieve optimal voltage regulation, preventing logic resets when the heavy actuators draw transient currents.

Interconnect Framework: Custom 10-conductor flexible ribbon cables handle the routing across the articulated joints of the glove. This eliminates structural bulk, ensures signal integrity for weak analog sensor lines, and organizes power delivery into a single, scannable bus.

4.3 The Four Core Functional Modules

The intelligence and interactivity of the glove rely on three primary modules: Force Sensing (Input). Vibration Feedback (Tactile Output), and TENS Feedback (Neural Output).

Module 1: Finger Flexion Tracking Subsystem (Flex Sensors)

To track hand closure, finger rehabilitation angles, and range of motion (ROM), flexible Flex Sensors are integrated along the dorsal aspect of the glove’s fingers.
Operating Principle: The sensor consists of a flexible substrate with carbon resistive elements. As the sensor is mechanically bent (flexed by the patient's fingers), its nominal resistance increases proportionally to the bend angle, varying from a baseline resistance of around 10 kΩ up to over 100 kΩ at maximum flexion.

Electrical Conditioning: Because the ESP32 microcontroller samples voltage rather than resistance, the Flex Sensor is placed into a classic voltage divider circuit with a static  resistor. The output analog voltage  is governed by the following equation:


ESP32 Connection: The conditioned analog voltage  is routed directly to a dedicated internal Analog-to-Digital Converter (ADC) pin of the ESP32 (e.g., GPIO 34). The microcontroller converts this analog voltage into a 12-bit digital value ranging from 0 to 4095, which is then dynamically mapped within the firmware to display the exact flexion status of the patient's fingers.


Module 2: Vibration Haptic Feedback
Direct Actuation and Driving Circuit: The eccentric rotating mass (ERM) vibration motor is powered directly via the 5V DC rail. The digital trigger signal is managed by a MOSFET driver module that interfaces with the ESP32 microcontroller, ensuring a fast, clean, and stable current switching path to actuate the haptic response.

Module 3: TENS Electro-Tactile Feedback
Pulse Generation and Modulation Architecture: The Transcutaneous Electrical Nerve Stimulation (TENS) module processes digital control signals generated by the ESP32:
The dedicated TENS board draws its primary operating power from the main power distribution board to handle high-voltage amplification.
The ESP32 implements precise Pulse Chopping and Modulation algorithms to manipulate the frequencies and pulse widths of the electrical signals.
These chopped pulses are sent through a MOSFET driver stage for electrical isolation and safe delivery via the therapeutic cables to the Conductive Electrodes/Pads attached to the patient's skin.
TENS Operating Modes: The firmware supports Three Distinct Therapeutic Modes that can be dynamically toggled via the user interface:
Conventional Mode (Continuous high frequency, low intensity)
Burst Mode (Intermittent packets of high-frequency pulses)
Modulated Mode (Cyclically varying pulse width or frequency) (Note: The accompanying Arduino firmware fully implements this dynamic state machine via the switch(page) controller function).

Module 4: Grip Force Measurement Subsystem
Digital Force Measurement Principle: To provide an objective clinical evaluation of the patient's recovery, a Flex Force/Force Sensing Resistor (FSR) is utilized in a classic Voltage Divider Circuit, routed directly to the analog-to-digital converter (ADC) inputs of the ESP32.
Percentage Scale Feedback: The raw analog voltage changes caused by the patient's grip are mapped in the firmware into a clear Percentage Scale (0% - 100%):
Clinical Progress Indicator: The percentage feedback allows for a granular, real-time assessment of hand strength (e.g., a light touch registers a minimal percentage, while an intense grip scales up toward 100%). This provides both the patient and the physician with precise metrics on muscle fiber activation and hand recovery stages.
Mechanical Testing Interface: For structural stability and ergonomics, the FSR sensor is physically mounted onto a customized Rehabilitation Ball. The patient squeezes this ball during assessment sessions, ensuring an even distribution of grip forces and perfectly simulating natural grasping mechanics during rehabilitation. (This setup aligns structurally with the physical configuration seen in the project photos, featuring the blue training ball centered within the hand).

4.4 Physical Integration and System Implementation

Electronics Consolidation (PCB Assembly)

The entire electrical network is assembled onto a unified prototyping motherboard:

The ESP32 is mounted on the left, easily accessible for programming via its micro-USB interface.

The lower section houses three high-current MOSFET/transistor driver blocks (marked by blue terminal blocks) which safely distribute power to the high-load pneumatic valves, pump, and vibration arrays.

The buck converter regulates the input power right at the entrance point, distributing a clean, filtered 5V rail across the board.

The Mechanical Glove Testbed

The electronics link directly to the physical glove frame:

Pneumatic Actuation: A heavy-duty DC pressure pump and a series of three-way solenoid valves route airflow through clear manifolds into the corrugated pneumatic actuators running along the fingers. This provides global flexion resistance.

Feedback Distribution: The FSR sensors reside at the structural fingertips to measure grip force. The vibration motors are located at the joints for rapid alerts, and the TENS electrodes reside in the Paim area for deep sensory stimulation.

Closed-Loop Firmware Execution

The firmware runs a continuous feedback control loop. When the user closes their hand, the Force Sensing Module tracks the real-time pressure increase. if the force passes a software threshold, the ESP32 triggers the Pneumatic Subsystem to restrict movement, while simultaneously scaling up the pulse widths of the Vibration Module and TENS Module. This provides a highly immersive sense of touching a solid object.

3. Detailed Test Procedures for Critical Components
The following components are identified as high-risk / high-impact — their failure would compromise system functionality or safety.

3.1 Solenoid/Pneumatic Valve
Test Objective – Verify electrical activation, mechanical response, and absence of short circuits.
Equipment
DC power supply (rated voltage, e.g., 12 V / 24 V)
Digital multimeter (DMM) with continuity and resistance modes
Oscilloscope (optional, for coil inrush measurement)
Stopwatch
Procedure

Conclusions

Summary of the "Smart Rehabilitation Glove" Project
1. Core Idea & Problem Statement:This project aims to design and develop a smart, wearable electronic glove to assist patients suffering from neurological and musculoskeletal hand impairments (e.g., stroke survivors). The main challenge in hand rehabilitation is overcoming limited range of motion, muscle weakness, sensory loss, chronic pain, and reduced dexterity—all of which severely impact patients' ability to perform daily activities and maintain their independence.
2. Proposed Solution (The Smart Glove):The glove offers an integrated diagnostic and therapeutic system that combines treatment with objective measurement. It is built around three core functional modules:
TENS Module (Transcutaneous Electrical Nerve Stimulation): For manual pain relief and reduction of muscle spasticity.
Vibration Therapy Module: For sensory stimulation to enhance neuromuscular response.
Force Measurement Module: Using high-precision sensors to provide real-time, digital feedback on grip strength, enabling quantitative progress tracking.
3. Development Methodology:A systematic, top-down design and bottom-up integration approach was followed, consisting of:
Analysis & Requirements Specification: Defined in consultation with physiotherapy references.
Theoretical Design: The system was divided into four layers (Sensing, Processing, Actuation, and Interface).
Modular Assembly & Unit Testing: Each component (power supply, controller, valves, sensors) was tested independently.
Full System Integration: All modules were assembled into a unified housing with embedded firmware.
Functional Verification & Calibration: The complete system was tested under simulated real-world conditions, with iterative tuning of sensors and valve timing for smooth, natural movement.
4. Key Technical Components:The system integrates several electronic and mechanical parts, including:
ESP32 Microcontroller: The central processing unit that reads sensor data, executes control logic, and drives actuators.
Pneumatic Glove: Contains inflatable air actuators that assist with finger flexion and extension.
Power Supply & Regulation Modules: To provide stable voltages (3.3V, 5V, 12V) to all components.
MOSFET Modules: Act as high-speed electronic switches to safely drive high-current devices (valves and motors) using low-voltage ESP32 signals.
Display Screen: A visual user interface showing real-time data (force, valve status, vibration intensity).
Control Panel: Physical buttons and switches for manual operation.
5. Testing and Validation Strategy:A rigorous three-stage testing plan was implemented to ensure reliability and safety:
Component-level Testing: Verifying individual parts (e.g., solenoid valves, MOSFETs, force sensors, pneumatic glove airtightness) against datasheet specifications.
Sub-system Integration Testing: Checking interactions between groups (e.g., ESP32 + MOSFET + valve).
System-level Validation: Simulating end-to-end clinical use scenarios (e.g., force-triggered inflation with display feedback).
Each test had clear pass/fail criteria (e.g., valve switching time < 50 ms, ADC change ≥ 500 counts for force sensor, pressure drop ≤ 5% over 60 seconds), and results were logged for traceability.
6. Achievement of Objectives:The project successfully met its primary goals:
Restoring Range of Motion (ROM): Via pneumatic-assisted finger movements.
Increasing Strength & Dexterity: Through resistive exercises and force feedback.
Non-Invasive Pain Management: Using TENS as a safer alternative to medication.
Enhancing Activities of Daily Living (ADLs): Empowering patients to perform self-care and fine motor tasks.
Improving Coordination & Proprioception: Through combined vibration and electrical stimulation.
Providing Customized Treatment Plans: Therapists can adjust intensity based on objective data, ensuring a personalized recovery path.
7. Achievements and Future Work:A functional prototype was successfully developed, integrating sensing, actuation, and feedback in a single wearable device. Future enhancements could include:
Developing a more interactive user interface (mobile app or PC software).
Adding wireless communication (Wi-Fi/Bluetooth) for remote monitoring and data logging.
Conducting extensive clinical trials to validate therapeutic efficacy across larger patient groups.
Optimizing the mechanical design for greater comfort, lighter weight, and improved aesthetics.
