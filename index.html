Abstract

An Integrated Therapeutic System

This project proposes a smart wearable rehabilitation glove for patients with neurological and musculoskeletal hand impairments — offering real-time progress tracking alongside multi-modal therapy.

TENS Module
Transcutaneous Electrical Nerve Stimulation for pain modulation. Three modes: Conventional, Burst, and Modulated.
Vibration Therapy Module
ERM haptic actuators providing sensory stimulation to enhance neuromuscular response via precise PWM control.
Force Measurement Module
FSR sensors mapped to 0–100% grip percentage — enabling quantitative, real-time clinical assessment of hand recovery.
Clinical Value

Empowers therapists to monitor patients' progress quantitatively and adjust treatment intensity based on objective data — ensuring a personalized and documented recovery path.

Problem Statement

Core Challenges of Hand Rehabilitation

Overcoming functional and physical deficits that arise from injuries, diseases, or neurological conditions affecting the hand.

Limited Range of Motion

Fractures and arthritis restrict joint mobility, impairing the ability to grasp, pinch, or perform essential movements.

Muscle Atrophy & Weakness

Prolonged immobilization leads to muscle wasting, significantly reducing grip strength and endurance.

Sensory Deficits

Nerve damage alters tactile feedback (touch, temperature, pressure), making safe object manipulation difficult.

Impaired Coordination

Disrupted motor skills make fine, precise tasks such as writing, typing, or handling small objects extremely challenging.

Chronic Pain

Persistent pain acts as a major barrier, preventing patients from utilizing their hand effectively in daily life.

Our Smart Solution

An integrated wearable glove combining pneumatic actuation, TENS, vibration therapy, and real-time force measurement.

Historical Background

Evolution of Hand Rehabilitation

From ancient splinting to modern smart wearables — a journey spanning centuries of medical innovation.

Ancient Era
Ancient Beginnings

Early societies used primitive therapeutic methods like splinting and joint movement exercises in ancient Egypt to manage hand injuries.

18th–19th Century
Surgical Pioneers

Guillaume Dupuytren pioneered operative procedures for correcting hand deformities, establishing principles of modern rehabilitation.

World Wars
Wartime Innovation

Severe trauma catalyzed rapid advances: tendon rerouting, custom splint fabrication, and functional electrical stimulation (FES).

1950s–1977
Formal Specialty

Hand rehabilitation emerged as a formal specialty. The American Society of Hand Therapists (ASHT) was founded in 1977.

Today 2026

System Architecture

4-Layer Design
Philosophy

The system was decomposed into four primary architecture layers for modularity, reliability, and ease of testing.

Sensing
Input & Sensing Layer
Flex sensors (finger ROM) + FSR sensors (grip force). 12-bit ADC readings on dedicated GPIO pins.
↓
Processing
Processing & Control Layer
ESP32 dual-core (LX6): Core 1 for analog sampling & safety, Core 0 for PWM & wireless telemetry.
↓
Actuation
Actuation & Power Layer
Solenoid valves + pneumatic glove + MOSFET switching. Buck converter (LM2596) for stable 5V/12V rails.
↓
Interface

Materials & Methods — Core Components

Control, Power & Actuation

The electronic and mechanical backbone that drives the smart rehabilitation system.

Controller
ESP32 Microcontroller

Dual-core LX6 processor. Handles sensor reading, control logic, PWM generation, and wireless communication simultaneously.

Actuation
Pneumatic Glove

Wearable component with inflatable air actuators along each finger. Assists finger flexion and extension for stroke rehabilitation.

Interface
LCD Display Screen

Shows real-time system data: pressure levels, force %, valve status (open/closed), vibration intensity, and diagnostics.

Switching
MOSFET Module

High-speed electronic switch. Allows low-voltage ESP32 signal to safely control high-current loads (valves, motors) via PWM.

Power
Power Supply Adaptor

Converts 220V AC to DC (12V/5V). Protects circuit from voltage fluctuations and powers all electronic components safely.

Regulation
Power Supply Module (LM2596)

DC-DC step-down converter. Distributes stable voltages (3.3V, 5V, 12V) to match each component's specific power needs.

Materials & Methods — Sensors & Therapy

Sensing, Feedback & Therapy

The therapeutic and sensory modules that bring the glove's intelligence to life.

Therapy
TENS Cable & Electrodes

Transmits electrical impulses for targeted muscle stimulation. Supports Conventional, Burst, and Modulated therapeutic modes.

Haptic
Vibration Motor (ERM)

Eccentric Rotating Mass actuator at fingertips and joints. Provides tactile feedback simulating texture, friction, and impact.

Sensing
Force Sensor (FSR)

Thin flexible sensor measuring grip pressure mapped to 0–100% scale. Mounted on a rehabilitation ball for natural grasping assessment.

Pneumatic
Solenoid Valves

Electromechanical air valves controlling compressed airflow. ESP32 signals open/close them for precise glove inflation/deflation.

Regulation
Voltage Regulator 7805

Converts 7–35V input into a stable fixed +5V DC output. Provides clean, ripple-free voltage for the ESP32, display, and sensors.

Pressure
Air Pressure Sensor HX710B

24-bit ADC chip measuring pneumatic pressure (0–40 kPa) for closed-loop control — auto-stops inflation at target pressure.

Dev Methodology (1/2)

6-Phase Top-Down
Approach

A systematic top-down design and bottom-up integration methodology — Part 1: Design to Unit Testing.

1
Analysis & Requirements
Defined functional & non-functional requirements in consultation with physiotherapy references (ROM, force, exercise scenarios).
2
Theoretical Design
System decomposed into 4 layers. Block diagrams and communication protocols (GPIO, PWM, UART) defined.
3
Modular Assembly & Unit Testing
Power supply → control circuit → pneumatic actuation → display interface tested independently before integration.
Dev Methodology (2/2)

Integration &
Calibration

A systematic top-down design and bottom-up integration methodology — Part 2: Integration to Final Tuning.

4
Full System Integration
All modules assembled into final mechanical housing. Unified firmware managing all tasks concurrently via ESP32 timers and interrupts.
5
Functional Verification
Full system tested under real-world clinical conditions: valve response time, force sensor accuracy, stability over multiple cycles.
6
Calibration & Final Tuning
Force sensor offset/gain adjusted. Valve timing fine-tuned for smooth, natural finger trajectories. Display optimized for clinical clarity.
Firmware & Software

Developed using Arduino IDE with C++. The ESP32 utilizes FreeRTOS capabilities on its dual cores to manage sensor polling, display updating, and wireless communication concurrently without blocking.

Hardware & Mechanical

Mechanical design and housing fabrication supported by CAD software (SolidWorks) for structural layout planning, ensuring a compact, ergonomic, and patient-friendly wearable format.

Testing & Validation

Rigorous 3-Stage
Test Strategy

All tests follow the V-model — each test case maps directly to a specific system requirement with pass/fail criteria.

ST1
Component-Level Testing (Unit)
13 components tested individually: MOSFET switching, 7805 regulation, valve activation, FSR linearity, HX710B pressure response, pneumatic airtightness.
ST2
Sub-System Integration Testing
Group interactions verified: ESP32 + MOSFET + Valve, ESP32 + FSR + Display, Power + Regulator chain. All protocols validated via oscilloscope.
ST3
System-Level Validation (End-to-End)
Complete clinical scenario: FSR press → valve open → glove inflate → pressure monitor → auto shutoff → display update. Looped 10+ cycles.
